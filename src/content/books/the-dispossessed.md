Le Guin builds two worlds -- the anarchist moon Anarres and the capitalist planet Urras -- and refuses to let either be a simple answer. Shevek, the physicist protagonist, discovers that his supposedly free society has developed invisible walls: social pressure, intellectual gatekeeping, conformity disguised as solidarity.

The dual timeline structure works beautifully. You read the Anarres chapters with growing disillusionment and the Urras chapters with growing unease, until both timelines converge in a moment that is neither triumph nor defeat.

## Quotes

> You cannot buy the revolution. You cannot make the revolution. You can only be the revolution.

> The wall is the thing that separates us from our freedom.

## Notes

- This is the most honest political novel I have read. Le Guin clearly sympathizes with the anarchist project but refuses to romanticize it.
- The physics in the book (simultaneity theory) maps loosely to the ansible, which shows up in her other Hainish novels.
- Read back-to-back with "The Left Hand of Darkness" for the full range of her political imagination.
